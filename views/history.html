Overview

The Duquesne Incline is a historic funicular railway in Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania, that has been in operation since 1877. It was originally constructed to transport workers who lived on Mount Washington down to the factories and mills along the Monongahela River. At the time, Pittsburgh’s steep hills made commuting difficult, and inclines like Duquesne (along with others) provided a practical solution. The incline was designed by Samuel Diescher, a renowned engineer known for his work on multiple funiculars across the U.S. By the mid-20th century, as automobiles and roads became more common, the incline faced potential closure. However, in 1963, a community-led restoration effort saved it, ensuring its continued operation. Today, it serves as both a historic landmark and a practical form of public transit, offering breathtaking views of downtown Pittsburgh.

Timeline

  • 1877 – The Duquesne Incline officially opens to the public.
  • 1901 – The incline is electrified, replacing the original steam-powered system.
  • 1940s-1950s – The incline faces declining ridership as automobiles become more common.
  • 1963 – The incline is nearly shut down but is saved by a community fundraising effort.
  • 1975 – The incline is added to the National Register of Historic Places, solidifying its status as a historic landmark.

Duquesne Incline Today

Today, the Duquesne Incline is one of Pittsburgh’s most iconic attractions, offering stunning panoramic views of the city skyline. It operates daily and serves both commuters and tourists. Visitors can explore a small museum at the upper station, which showcases the incline’s history and mechanical workings. The ride itself, at 800 feet long and 400 feet high, remains an engineering marvel.
